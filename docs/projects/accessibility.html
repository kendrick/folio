Accessibility Rehabilitation

Role: Research, Consensus Building, Collaboration

As UXers, we’re often quick to remind others what strong advocates of the user we are. And that’s as it should be! But in 2016, after hearing a keynote from the inimitable Léonie Watson, I was shaken awake to an empathy gap that I had been blissfully ignorant of: users of assistive technology.

It’s trivially easy to break a site’s accessibility, and my own designs had been doing so for years. I sought to influence the organization to repair the accessibility of our e-commerce site, armed with a newfound awareness for users with visual, motor, or other impairments.

This was a tougher sell within the company than I expected. One of the first questions I heard was “Why would a blind person even shop our site?” Developers and directors alike were sure it wasn’t worth the effort.

I tried to explain that there was both a carrot and a stick: if our site was easy to shop by users of assisive technology, it would set us apart; conversely if we eschewed accessibility, we risked potential legal action against us.

In the winter of 2016 I put a talk together and presented it to a room full of developers, VPs, and others from across the organization. I explained the legal risk that I had researched and demonstrated what it was like to navigate via keyboard or use a screen reader on our site. I showed how easy it would be to fix some of the low-hanging fruit. And it worked.

Driven more by the stick than the carrot, the product team prioritized the work. We engaged with a third party, the best in class when it comes to accessibility, to audit our site and find the issues we couldn’t.

I’m now the company-wide subject matter expert on accessibility, and consider myself a fierce a11y advocate.

The most satisfying part of this long journey is that nearly everyone involved in e-commerce development are mindful of accessibility! There's a true sense of empathy within the dev team and within the business.

Our entire shopping path is fully accessible and compliant with WCAG 2.0, apart from alt text on all of our imagery—and we’re designing a machine learning-based solution for that, which should be ready this summer.
